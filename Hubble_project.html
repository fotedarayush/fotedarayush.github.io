Estimating the Hubble Constant via Gravitational-Wave Dark Sirens

Precise determination of the Hubble constant (H₀) remains a central puzzle in cosmology, owing to persistent disagreements between early-Universe (CMB) and late-Universe (supernovae, Cepheids) measurements. In this project, I explore a completely independent route: using gravitational-wave observations of binary black-hole mergers—so-called “dark sirens”—to infer H₀ without relying on any electromagnetic counterpart.

Aims

  • Validate a likelihood-based framework for statistically associating each gravitational-wave event with its host galaxy.
  • Recover frequentist point estimates of H₀ by summing and maximizing joint likelihoods across many events.
  • Quantify uncertainties via Monte Carlo resampling and by constructing a full Bayesian posterior.

Methods

  • I generate distance measurements with 30% Gaussian uncertainty and sky localizations via a von Mises–Fisher model (5° spread).
  • Candidate hosts are drawn from the EAGLE simulation, filtered by stellar mass to mimic survey completeness.
  • For each merger, I compute joint angular × distance likelihoods over all galaxies, then combine them across N = 100 events.
  • Frequentist estimates come from maximizing the summed likelihood, while Monte Carlo resampling builds confidence intervals.
  • I also form a Bayesian posterior by multiplying per-event likelihoods and applying a uniform prior on H₀ ∈ [40, 100] km s⁻¹ Mpc⁻¹.

Results

Over four independent realizations (100 events each), our recovered H₀ values cluster between 68–72 km s⁻¹ Mpc⁻¹, bracketing the true input of 67 km s⁻¹ Mpc⁻¹. The combined Bayesian posterior yields H₀ = 72.0−19.8+17.9 km s⁻¹ Mpc⁻¹ (68% credible interval), covering ≈ 60% of the prior range. Spline smoothing effectively reduces Monte Carlo noise in our likelihood curves.

Conclusions

Even without electromagnetic counterparts, gravitational-wave dark sirens can deliver competitive constraints on the Universe’s expansion rate. Our hybrid frequentist + Bayesian + Monte Carlo approach provides a robust framework. Scaling to the hundreds or thousands of future detections and leveraging deeper galaxy catalogs will be key to resolving the “Hubble tension” in the next generation of gravitational-wave cosmology.
