Beginner’s Guide to General Relativity

A streamlined, two‑layer introduction: first the basics (intuition + minimal equations), then four mathematical parts you can expand inline. Equations are typeset with MathJax.

Basics (One‑Page Overview)

General Relativity (GR) says gravity is not a force but spacetime geometry. A metric \(g_{\mu\nu}\) measures intervals ("distances") via \(\mathrm d s^2 = g_{\mu\nu}\,\mathrm d x^{\mu}\,\mathrm d x^{\\nu}\). Free particles follow geodesics (the straightest possible worldlines), determined by the metric’s connection \(\Gamma^{\rho}{}_{\mu\nu}\). Spacetime curvature—encoded by the Riemann tensor—is sourced by energy and momentum according to Einstein’s field equations \[ G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}\,T_{\mu\nu}. \] In the weak‑field, slow‑motion limit this reproduces Newton’s gravity; in strong fields it predicts black holes, gravitational waves, and an expanding Universe.

Part I — Elements of Differential Geometry
Euclidean Geometry

Goal: Set up coordinates and vectors in flat (Euclidean) space and introduce the notation used throughout GR.

Conventions & Notation

  • Indices: Roman i, j, k label spatial components (1,2,3,…,n). Greek μ, ν, σ will later label spacetime (0,1,2,3).
  • Einstein summation: repeated indices are summed, e.g. \(A = x^j e_j = x^1 e_1 + x^2 e_2 + \cdots\).
  • Upper vs lower indices are labels, not powers: \(x^\mu\) is a component, not “\(x\) to a power”.

Basis in \(\mathbb{E}^3\)

Standard Cartesian basis: \(\mathbf{i}=(1,0,0),\ \mathbf{j}=(0,1,0),\ \mathbf{k}=(0,0,1)\). A point has position vector \(\mathbf{r}=x\,\mathbf{i}+y\,\mathbf{j}+z\,\mathbf{k}\). In \(n\)D we choose a linearly-independent set \(\mathcal{B}=\{e_1,\dots,e_n\}\); if \(x^1 e_1+\cdots + x^n e_n=0\) then \(x^1=\cdots=x^n=0\).

Contravariant & Covariant Components (Natural vs Dual Basis)

In a (possibly skew) coordinate system \((u,v)\) with natural basis \(\{e_1\equiv e_u,\ e_2\equiv e_v\}\), a vector expands as \[ \mathbf{C}=C^i e_i = C^1 e_1 + C^2 e_2, \] where \(C^i\) are contravariant components (often shown as a column vector).

The dual basis \(\{e^1, e^2\}\) satisfies \(e^i\!\cdot e_j=\delta^i{}_j\). Using it we also have the covariant expansion \[ \mathbf{C}=C_i e^i, \qquad C_i=(C_1, C_2)\ \text{(row vector)}. \] In orthonormal Cartesian coordinates the natural and dual bases coincide, which hides this distinction in elementary vector calculus.

Metric Tensor, Inner Product & Kronecker Delta

The metric encodes lengths and angles: \[ g_{ij}=e_i\cdot e_j,\qquad g^{ij}=e^i\cdot e^j,\qquad g_{ij}\,g^{jk}=\delta_i{}^{k}. \] For any \(\lambda,\mu\), the scalar product can be written \[ \lambda\cdot\mu = g_{ij}\,\lambda^i\,\mu^j = \lambda_i\,\mu^i. \]

Line Element

Parametrise a curve by coordinates \(u^i(t)\). The infinitesimal displacement is \( \mathrm{d}\mathbf{s}=e_i\,\mathrm{d}u^i\), and the squared length is the line element: \[ \boxed{\ \mathrm{d}s^2=g_{ij}\,\mathrm{d}u^i\,\mathrm{d}u^j\ }. \]

Examples

  • Cartesian (2D/3D): \(\mathrm{d}s^2=\mathrm{d}x^2+\mathrm{d}y^2\) (2D) or \(\mathrm{d}s^2=\mathrm{d}x^2+\mathrm{d}y^2+\mathrm{d}z^2\) (3D). Hence \(g_{ij}=\mathrm{diag}(1,1)\) or \(\mathrm{diag}(1,1,1)\).
  • Plane polar (2D): with \(x=r\cos\theta,\ y=r\sin\theta\), \[ \mathrm{d}x=\cos\theta\,\mathrm{d}r - r\sin\theta\,\mathrm{d}\theta,\qquad \mathrm{d}y=\sin\theta\,\mathrm{d}r + r\cos\theta\,\mathrm{d}\theta, \] which gives \[ \mathrm{d}s^2=\mathrm{d}r^2 + r^2\,\mathrm{d}\theta^2,\qquad g_{ij}=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\[2pt]0&r^2\end{pmatrix}. \]

Key Takeaways

  • Natural (basis) vs dual basis matters away from orthonormal frames; components with upper vs lower indices transform differently.
  • The metric \(g_{ij}\) and its inverse \(g^{ij}\) relate covariant and contravariant components and define lengths/angles.
  • \(\delta_i{}^{k}\) is the identity that results from \(g_{ij}g^{jk}=\delta_i{}^{k}\).
