What is GR?

General Relativity (GR) is Einstein’s theory of gravity. Instead of a force acting at a distance, gravity is the curvature of spacetime. Matter tells spacetime how to curve; curved spacetime tells matter how to move.

“Spacetime” = 3D space + time as a unified 4D arena. Distances in spacetime are measured by the metric, which generalizes the Pythagorean theorem to include time.

Core ideas

  • Equivalence principle: Locally, you can’t distinguish uniform acceleration from a gravitational field. In a freely falling elevator, physics looks like it does in gravity‑free space.
  • Geometry, not force: Free‑falling objects follow the straightest possible paths (geodesics) in curved spacetime.
  • Curvature from matter/energy: Mass–energy (including pressure) curves spacetime; curvature feeds back into motion.
  • Local vs global: Over tiny regions, spacetime looks flat; globally it can be curved (like a sphere looks flat on small patches).

From Special Relativity to General Relativity

Special Relativity (SR) unifies space and time and replaces absolute time with invariant spacetime intervals. GR keeps SR’s local structure but allows the metric to vary from place to place due to gravity.

  • SR uses the flat Minkowski metric: ds² = -c²dt² + dx² + dy² + dz².
  • GR uses a position‑dependent metric gμν(x) so that ds² = gμν dxμ dxν.
  • Curvature is encoded in derivatives of gμν}; when curvature vanishes you recover SR.

Free fall & geodesics

In GR, the trajectory of a freely falling particle extremizes the spacetime interval—it’s a geodesic. In flat space, geodesics are straight lines; on a sphere, they’re great circles; in curved spacetime, they’re the natural generalization.

  • Light follows null geodesics (ds² = 0), massive particles follow timelike geodesics (ds² < 0 in the (-,+,+,+) convention).
  • Gravity doesn’t “pull”; rather, spacetime geometry guides motion.

Einstein’s field equation (without tears)

The heart of GR relates curvature to energy–momentum:

Gμν = (8πG/c⁴) Tμν

  • Gμν: built from the metric and its derivatives; it captures spacetime curvature.
  • Tμν: energy–momentum tensor of matter/fields (includes energy density, momentum flow, pressure).
  • The equation says: energy and momentum shape the geometry.

You don’t need the full tensor machinery to appreciate GR’s predictions; many classic results follow from symmetry and simple limits.

Classic tests

  • Mercury’s perihelion advance: GR predicts a small extra precession (~43″/century) beyond Newtonian gravity.
  • Deflection of starlight: Light bends near the Sun; first measured during the 1919 eclipse.
  • Gravitational redshift & time dilation: Clocks lower in a gravitational potential run slightly slower.
  • Shapiro delay: Light takes extra time to pass near massive bodies due to curvature.

Black holes & gravitational waves

Black holes

  • Schwarzschild black hole: Non‑rotating, spherically symmetric. Event horizon at r = 2GM/c².
  • Kerr black hole: Rotating; has an ergosphere and frame dragging.

Gravitational waves

Ripples in spacetime produced by accelerating masses (e.g., merging black holes). They travel at the speed of light and carry energy. LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA detect these tiny strains, opening a new window on the Universe.

Cosmology in one page

Assuming the Universe is homogeneous and isotropic on large scales leads to the FLRW metric and the Friedmann equations. The scale factor a(t) encodes expansion; the Hubble parameter is H = \dot a/a.

  • Different energy contents (matter, radiation, dark energy) drive different expansion histories.
  • Distances (comoving, luminosity, angular diameter) follow from the metric and a(t).
