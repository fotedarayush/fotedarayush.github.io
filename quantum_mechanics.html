Quantum Mechanics: A Gentle Start

An intuition‑first tour of core concepts: wave–particle duality, Schrödinger’s equation, probability, tunnelling, multi‑D wells & degeneracy, the harmonic oscillator, and the leap to spherical coordinates & spherical harmonics.

Wave–Particle Duality

Quantum objects behave like waves and particles. De Broglie linked momentum and wavelength:

p = \hbar k = h/\lambda, E = \hbar\,\omega

Interference patterns (e.g., electron double‑slit) show that probability amplitudes add like waves; measured hits arrive as particles.

Schrödinger’s Equation

In 1D the time‑dependent equation is

i\hbar\,\partial_t\psi(x,t) = \left[-\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2m}\,\partial_x^2 + V(x,t)\right]\psi(x,t)

For time‑independent potentials, separate variables: \psi(x,t)=e^{-iEt/\hbar}\,u(x). Then

-\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2m}u''(x)+V(x)u(x)=E u(x)

Physical solutions are single‑valued, finite, continuous (with justified exceptions in the derivative), and normalisable.

Probability & Normalisation

  • Probability density: P(x,t)=|\psi(x,t)|^2. For stationary states: P(x)=|u(x)|^2.
  • Normalisation: \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|u(x)|^2\,dx = 1.
  • Expectation of an operator \hat O: \langle O\rangle=\int u^*(x)\,\hat O\,u(x)\,dx.

Uncertainty Principle

Wave nature implies intrinsic spreads:

\Delta x\,\Delta p \ge \tfrac{\hbar}{2}, \Delta t\,\Delta E \ge \tfrac{\hbar}{2}

Localising a wave packet in space demands many momenta (Fourier components), increasing \Delta p.

Tunnelling through a Barrier

For energy E<V_0 and barrier width b, solutions decay inside the barrier yet yield non‑zero transmission T. In the large‑barrier limit,

T \propto e^{-2\alpha b} with \alpha = \sqrt{\tfrac{2m}{\hbar^2}(V_0-E)}.

For E>V_0 you get oscillatory solutions inside, resonances, and energy‑dependent T.

From 1D to 2D/3D: Infinite Wells

In multiple dimensions the time‑independent equation is

-\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2m}\,\nabla^2 u(\mathbf r) + V(\mathbf r)u(\mathbf r) = E\,u(\mathbf r)

  • If V(\mathbf r)=V_x(x)+V_y(y)+V_z(z), the wavefunction separates: u= X(x)Y(y)Z(z).
  • Rectangular infinite wells in 2D/3D yield discrete energies with independent quantum numbers per axis.

Degeneracy & Symmetry

Symmetries of the potential produce degenerate energy levels (different states, same energy). For a square 2D well (a=b) or isotropic 3D boxes, states like (n_x,n_y)=(1,2) and (2,1) are degenerate. Linear combinations are equally valid and often reveal the symmetry more clearly.

Harmonic Oscillator (1D, 2D, 3D)

  • 1D: V=\tfrac12 m\omega^2 x^2, energies E_n=(n+\tfrac12)\hbar\omega, eigenstates involve Hermite polynomials.
  • 3D isotropic: V=\tfrac12 m\omega^2(x^2+y^2+z^2), energies E_{n_x n_y n_z}=\hbar\omega(n_x+n_y+n_z+\tfrac32) with characteristic degeneracies.
  • 2D isotropic: zero‑point energy \hbar\omega; non‑isotropic cases split degeneracies.

Spherical Coordinates & Separation

For spherically symmetric potentials V(r), change variables and separate:

u(r,\theta,\phi)=R(r)Y(\theta,\phi)

The radial and angular parts decouple; the angular equation yields spherical harmonics.

Spherical Harmonics & Quantum Numbers

  • Angular solutions: Y_{\ell m}(\theta,\phi) with \ell\ge0 and -\ell\le m\le\ell.
  • They are orthonormal on the sphere and encode angular momentum quantisation; each \ell gives 2\ell+1 degenerate m states.

Radial Probability & Expectation Values

With u=R(r)Y(\theta,\phi) and normalised R, the radial probability density is

P(r)=r^2 |R(r)|^2

Expectations like \langle r\rangle follow from \int_0^\infty r\,P(r)\,dr. Maxima of P(r) give most‑probable radii (useful for oscillator and hydrogenic problems).
